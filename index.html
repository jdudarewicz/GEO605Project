Addressing Simpson's Perspective

Jeffery Simpson, The Globe and Mail

“ Large elements of aboriginal Canada live intellectually in a dream palace, a more comfortable place than where they actually reside ”

- Jefferey Simpson

Unfortunately while insensitive, on a surface level Simpson’s perspective on living within reserves seems to hold some truth.

Historically, the land that First Nations occupy now essentially is a government sanctioned displacement, meaning conditions from within them are less than ideal (Hanson). This lead to disruptions in First Nations, where social networks and kinship systems were disrupted in order to bring together arbitrary families into one community (Hanson). Aboriginal families tend to be extensive and complex, and the housing traditionally found on First Nations is not built to accommodate that, instead modeled after the typical American family (Hanson). These factors combined with marginalization from the capitalist economy, discriminatory legislation, and reduction of traditional lands, mean practices like fishing and hunting were not viable to support a First Nations family and result in poverty on reserves (Hanson).

The end result of reserves in their current form is that many of them suffer in extreme poverty due to the above highlighted external factors. For example, highlighted in the video to the left, Attawapiskat First Nation is suffering from a horrendous housing crisis. Many residents within this First Nation are living in overcrowded shacks and tents without a proper connection to electricity, a lack of running water, poor to non-existent insulation, and inadequate disposal of excreta; dumping human waste into ditches along the road (Angus). This results in a decrease of the communities overall health, increasing the risk of infectious diseases of the: respiratory tract, digestive tract; colds, ear infections, strep throat, sinus infections, bronchitis, along with other illnesses (Angus). As well, mental illnesses like depression and anxiety disorders run rampant just from living within these makeshift quarters (Angus). Regarding the more 'Americanized' dwellings, overcrowding, exposed fire stoves, and failing home materials lead to exposure to molds, high risk of fire and burns (Angus).

Example of poor living conditions of First Nations in Attawaspiskat

Where Simpson is incorrect lies in the idea that many Aboriginal Canadians are living in an intellectual dream palace- many are well aware of their circumstance. Yet, either through external factors enforced by socioeconomics, cultural genocide, social disruptions though legislation like the Indian Act, or economic and environmental challenges within the communities themselves (Hanson), there is little they can do to combat this as a community alone due to being overwhelmed by all these factors together.

As well, there are a plethora of reasons First Nations may want to live on reserve. Visiting the idle no more website, many reserves are based on culturally significant land (even if not spanning the entirety of land claims) where remaining within them preserves sovereignty, promotes protection of the land and waters from corporate pollution, as well as providing a space to honor traditions. These reserves have blossomed into a space where Aboriginal communities maintain their sense of community and cultural ties to continue their legacy (Hanson). They are free to practice cultural customs and promote teachings within reserve lands ideally without influence from European ideology and interference (Hanson). Disregarding the blatant issues that reserve living brings, they are one of the last beacons to preserve Aboriginal culture.

Prior to the intervention of European cultures, aboriginals had little difficulties living off of the land. Regarding access to foods, percentages varied between First Nations due to differing environments but plants, meats, and fish were staples of a native diet (Government of Canada). Game was easily hunted with spears, bows, and arrows, as well as traps and snares (Government of Canada). Northern hunters adapted to harsh environments by developing advanced routing fences to stampede animals into traps (Government of Canada). The Haudenosaunee were known to even grow crops like corn, beans, and squash, and would frequently relocate to grow in more fertile lands once current grounds were exhausted (Government of Canada). Plains First Nations primarily hunted buffalo, which was ingrained into their culture for thousands of years (Government of Canada). Food preservation was even a consideration- many meats, fish, and berries were dried in the summer, and within winter months stored up in trees with stripped bark to protect against scavengers (Government of Canada). The main takeaway is that prior to the intervention of Europeans, nomadic and settled First Nations could easily and comfortably survive on the lands existing food resources.

As for housing, it depended on whether a First Nation was nomadic or permanently resided. For nomadic First Nations, homes were built from either portable or easily erected materials to form tipis (Government of Canada). For more permanent nations, they generally opted to build structures like longhouses, Pacific Nations opted for more red cedar home-like structures, or within pit houses; a pit approximately two meters deep and six to twelve meter covered and insulated (Government of Canada). While many options were not as shielded as the American homes of current reservations, they were extremely in tune with the needs of the First Nation utilizing them and applied to cultures and customs surrounding them.

The issue with traditional living within the modern day revolves around how European settlers have interfered with the abilities of First Nations to continue on with their traditional ways of life. Among the largest points of contention are traditional land claims- many First Nations believe the federal government has unfairly taken or promised land and under delivered (Parrott and McCue). As well, modern day industrial activities interfere with the environment surrounding First Nations. For example, the 1002 Lands of the National Wildlife Refuge, home to the Porcupine Caribou Reproductive Lands, were recently opened to Oil and Gas drilling by the Republican Senate, meaning the Porcupine Caribou are at risk to effectively reproduce (Global Affairs Canada). This herd is one of particular importance to many First Nations in the area as it is both culturally and nutritionally significant, and is currently at high risk from habitat destruction (“Habitat”). While human nature is about adaptation, it is ridiculous to expect First Nations to adapt to such a cultural shift that was brought over by European settlers.

Directly addressing Simpsons point, I believe that there should be increased cooperation between the government and First Nations to help instill a traditional way of living if wanted by the community. While some may abuse this help, and potentially perpetuate lassitude, there is an aspect of obligation to provide for First Nations and ensure they can continue to operate to a traditional capacity. With the arrival of European settlers and the formation of Canada itself, we robbed these Nations of their land and nonconsensually forced a new way of living upon them they may not have asked for. We expect them to participate within the economy that we cultivate, yet deal them with a bad hand through systemic disadvantages like an uprooting of their culture. I believe this can be done through understanding and respect- through honoring their traditional land claims (Parrott and McCue) to the best of our abilities to allow them to live within a more traditional context, as well as providing a healthy level of economic support to fill in any gaps that may occur due to the modern need to participate in the world economy. Furthermore, a solution in which I will detail later into the website, is the introduction of efficient, safe, economically sensible, adaptable housing to revitalize northern living into a modern context.

What Constitutes Northern Sustainability?

To address Simpson's closing point:

" To imagine that isolated communities of a thousand or so people can be vibrant and self-sustaining, capable of discharging the panoply of responsibilities of “sovereignty,” is to live within the dream palace of memory. "

we must define what sustainability means in a Northern context. We can look at what we have currently covered when responding to Simpson, and relate to considerations which can be thought of as sustainable and unsustainable-

Poor, inefficient housing conditions which do not meet the needs of Canada’s First Nations put strain on First Nations ability to continue down stable path

  • Residential overcrowding (Angus) is linked to physical illness, particularly when paired with contagious diseases (L. Taylor)
  • Exposure to high and low temperatures due to inadequate shelter (Angus) can lead to adverse health effects, in particular cardiovascular anomalies (L. Taylor)
  • Those who are sensitive or allergic to mold, or are asthmatic, can experience intense reactions like fever, respiratory tract events, and shortness of breath when exposed in high concentrations. This does not include unproven adverse effects, which can be more severe if true (“CDC - Mold - General Information - Basic Facts.”)
  • Tainted drinking supplies in First Nations with water advisories mean increase risk of adverse health effects (Gester and Hessey)

All the above factors commonly found in many First Nations (Angus), along with inadequate access to health officials ("Health at a Glance") means unnecessary strain on a health network with an already abysmal capacity, which is can be considered unsustainable in the long run

Cultural ties on first nations lead to a strong sense of belonging ("Health at a Glance"), which can correlate to improved mental health and community.

This can be seen as a sustainable, desired outcome since it allows First Nations to maintain strong ties even through challenging conditions and situations.

Finally, we can give a concrete definition to sustainability within a Northern context consisting of ideas above as well as traditional ideas of sustainability.

A general definition of sustainability, by the Oxford Learner's Dictionary, is:

  1. the use of natural products and energy in a way that does not harm the environment
  2. the ability to continue or be continued for a long time

In the context of Northern communities, both sections of the definition are applicable. Being a steward to the natural in Northern Canada can perpetuate sustainability and allow future generations to enjoy both the nature and resources that those regions provide. As well, with a changing climate the north is particularly affected, so sustainability within the area is focused on its longevity to allow communities and ecosystems in the area to remain for a continued, long period of time.

As well, sustainability within a Northern context should involve adaptations to change where necessary, like highlighted in the Stockholm Environment Institute’s “Into the Blue” report. Failing to adapt to changes will result in communities being left behind.

So, overall, this report defines Northern Sustainability as:

  1. Avoiding and addressing harm to the environment in ways conscious to the context said environment
  2. Perpetuating cultures and ecosystems when possible
  3. Adapting to changes when necessary to uphold the above two core values

Homes Focused on Sustainability

To tackle sustainability in Northern communities, I am posing a solution focused particularly on addressing the ongoing housing crises in many First Nations communities. Starting with housing, we can address environmental, economic, and social factors of living within the north.

Generic Rectangle

The main inspiration for sustainable living involves the principle of tiny living. Living in a dorm room with approximately 100 square-footage of living space, I understand how efficient well-thought out space can be when purpose is applied. Obviously, my needs are very different to that of someone who lives in the North- my space is built for a single person to live in, versus the needs of a First Nation to house an entire community. As well, I live within a city with convenient access to grocers, education, and transportation which doesn’t exist in any community outside of a major city. I still believe that select principles of living within efficient, tiny-home like housing can apply to those living within the North.

First, lets cover the principles of efficient home living. While no set rules apply, a post by Liz on the blog “I Spy Plum Pie” goes over a couple staples of tiny homes.

  1. Have a purposeful space for everything
  2. Utilize multi-purpose essential products
  3. Make use of outdoor spaces

We can expand even further on these principles by introducing ideas involving environmental, economic, and social factors more suitable First Nations living while introducing aspects of sustainability:

  1. Inexpensive to manufacturer
  2. Utilize materials already available within the environment, or that are simple to transport in, or are are already transported in
  3. Suitable to the harsh, changing conditions of the north
  4. Modular, can be combined to create adaptable spaces to fit together families and communities

Combining the above, we can create a model utilizing a community like Attawapiskat.

Base design: shipping container home

Many First Nation members already receive deliveries of goods through means of a shipping container shipment, where a container they own is filled with items they order.

The idea is to turn these shipping containers into full fledged homes. Why?

Modularity

They can be standalone, or combined together to make larger structures (“14 Things to Know About Building with Shipping Containers”).

Affordability

A conventional wood-framed home on reserve can cost between $200-$300/sqft (Ross). A shipping container home can be manufactured for half of that amount (Ross), with prices of containers themselves only costing between $1400 - $5000 each (“Storage Container Pricing”).

Environmentally conscious

Infrastructure already exists to deliver these containers to First Nations, and any materials required to construct them unavailable at the site can be shipped within them. Any unused containers on site can be converted to new living spaces provided they are in a suitable condition.

Independent

Furthermore, many tiny homes can be run off-grid, since they utilize efficient appliances and unconventional solutions to sewage, electricity, and water yet still provide a comfortable living situation. The below video is an example of a particularly lavish, but tiny home- built for the cost of ~$125,000 CAD, while incurring minimal recurring costs:

New Zealand Tiny Home

Structurally Strong

Since shipping containers are built to be stacked on top of eachother while carrying immense amounts of cargo within them, they are more than apt to support the weight of home living (“14 Things to Know About Building with Shipping Containers”).

Proven

Shipping container homes are not a new solution to housing in First Nations. Pic Mobert First Nation has begun experimenting with shipping container homes, with solutions that expand from 1 to 3 bedroom dwellings (Ross). These homes are durable enough to withstand temperatures of -60 celcius, contain smart home features, as well as air circulation systems to remove potential mold inducing moisture, meaning they are both practical and modern (Ross). They are affordable too, meaning a load is taken off of both First Nations communities and government assistance.

Pic Mobert/SwissCan Partnership Shipping Container Home

Two Important Forms of Modularity

The containers themselves

As First Nations members rely on community and kinship (Hanson), shipping container homes can be connected in such a way that keeps the community close to each other. Through motifs like a common sub-community container for activities, degrees of separation can be maintained where the community feels like it lives together, but still contains spaces like bedrooms which convey privacy. These are not available in the traditional nuclear family homes built on reserves, since they are not built with the purpose to integrate communities, instead separating families.

The structures that support them

Since many First Nations are built on vulnerable land, like Attawapiskat which lies on a flood plain (Simpson), the structures which these homes lie on should be modular. That means, the homes should either lie on a structure that removes them from risk, or can be transported to exist outside of a risk zone. For shipping container homes in communities like Attawapiskat, they could exist on wooden risers which remove them from any indoor flood risk. For particularly severe events, these homes can then be removed from their risers, and transported away by tow bed, utilizing the inherent transportability of a shipping container (“14 Things to Know About Building with Shipping Containers”).

Environmentally Connected

Among the environmental aspects of shipping container living previously mentioned, shipping container living as a philosophy is connected with the environment itself. By living efficiently, less strain is placed on the surrounding ecosystem in terms of carbon output, as well as physical space. Furthermore, efficient living means a more cost effective lifestyle, opening up the ability to focus more traditional First Nations lifestyle as there is little focus on earning money to upkeep the home, which also puts less strain on the government if subsidizing the space is needed.
