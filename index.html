Introduction

“ Resilience is the capacity to buffer and adapt to stress and shocks, and thus navigate and shape change ”

- Stockholm Environment Institute

When initially approaching this project, this quote from the Stockholm Environment Institute inspired the direction I would take in responding to Simpsons editorial - that a sovereign, traditional lifestyle is attainable for First Nations for a low cost. My approach involves particularly investing in efficient environmentally friendly homes to tackle the continuing housing crisis (Angus), as well as suggesting ideas to make housing more adaptable to changing conditions particularly in the wake of the rising threat of climate change (Stockholm Environment Institute). For the first portion of this project, I will directly respond to Simpson’s editorial in which I will be arguing why I believe his perspective is flawed. Particularly: a majority of aboriginal Canada is not living in a dream palace; a low cost, traditional, sustainable way of living is attainable on reserve; why it makes sense for communities like Attawapiskat to remain where they are even with external risk factors; and to refute the notion that an isolated community cannot be vibrant and self sustaining. Then, I will be defining what I believe constitutes northern sustainability. Finally, I will discuss how I believe a redux in northern housing could improve environmental, economic, and social factors of living in the north.

Addressing Simpson's Perspective

Jeffery Simpson, The Globe and Mail

“ Large elements of aboriginal Canada live intellectually in a dream palace, a more comfortable place than where they actually reside ”

- Jefferey Simpson

Unfortunately while harsh, on face value Simpson’s perspective on living within reserves holds some truth.

Historically, the land that First Nations occupy now essentially is a government sanctioned displacement, meaning conditions from within them are less than ideal (Hanson). This lead to disruptions in First Nations, where social networks and kinship systems were disrupted in order to bring together arbitrary families into one community (Hanson). Aboriginal families tend to be extensive and complex, and the housing traditionally found on First Nations is not built to accommodate that, instead modeled after the typical Western family (Hanson). These factors combined with marginalization from the capitalist economy, discriminatory legislation, and reduction of traditional lands, mean practices like fishing and hunting were not viable to support a First Nations family and result in poverty on reserves (Hanson).
